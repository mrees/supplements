Magnesium

An essential mineral involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions — sleep, muscle, heart, and bone

Sleep Bone Health Heart Blood Pressure Muscle Anxiety
Evidence strength:
Strong (deficiency correction); Moderate (sleep, BP)

Overview

Magnesium is the fourth most abundant mineral in the human body and a cofactor in over 300 enzymatic reactions. It is involved in energy production (ATP synthesis), protein synthesis, DNA repair, nerve transmission, and muscle contraction. Approximately 60% of the body's magnesium is stored in bone.

Deficiency is very common in older adults due to reduced dietary intake, impaired gastrointestinal absorption, increased urinary losses (especially with diuretic use), and certain medications. The most bioavailable supplemental forms are magnesium glycinate, malate, and citrate.

Key fact for seniors Studies estimate that 70–80% of adults over 70 consume less magnesium than recommended. Low magnesium is associated with insulin resistance, higher cardiovascular risk, poor sleep quality, and accelerated bone loss.

What the Evidence Shows

Sleep quality

Magnesium has a role in regulating melatonin and GABA-mediated pathways. Several randomised trials in older adults with insomnia show that magnesium supplementation (especially magnesium glycinate) improves sleep onset, duration, and quality.

Blood pressure

Meta-analyses of randomised trials show a modest but significant reduction in systolic and diastolic blood pressure with magnesium supplementation, particularly in individuals who are deficient or hypertensive. Effects are most pronounced at doses above 370 mg/day.

Bone health

Magnesium is required for the activation of vitamin D and plays a direct role in bone mineral density. Low magnesium status is associated with osteoporosis. Supplementation appears to improve bone density in deficient older adults.

Cardiovascular health

Observational studies link adequate magnesium intake with lower risk of cardiovascular disease, atrial fibrillation, and type 2 diabetes. Intravenous magnesium is used clinically for acute arrhythmias, supporting the biological plausibility of a heart-protective role.

Muscle function and cramps

Magnesium is essential for normal muscle contraction and relaxation. Although evidence for treating leg cramps specifically is mixed, correcting deficiency reliably improves muscle function and reduces cramping in deficient individuals.

Safety & Cautions

Main side effect The most common side effect of magnesium supplements is diarrhoea, especially with magnesium oxide and citrate. Starting at a lower dose and titrating up reduces this risk. Magnesium glycinate is the best-tolerated form for older adults. Severe kidney disease (eGFR <30) is a contraindication, as the kidneys cannot excrete excess magnesium.
Drug interactions Magnesium can interfere with the absorption of certain antibiotics (fluoroquinolones, tetracyclines) and bisphosphonates — take at least 2 hours apart. Magnesium reduces the efficacy of some medications used for bone loss; check timing with a pharmacist.

Synergistic Combinations

  • Magnesium + Vitamin D: Magnesium is required to convert vitamin D into its active form. Without adequate magnesium, vitamin D supplementation may be less effective. This is one of the most important co-supplementation pairs in older adults.
  • Magnesium + Calcium: These two minerals work in opposing and complementary ways in muscle contraction and nerve signalling. A ratio of approximately 1:2 (magnesium:calcium) is generally recommended, though many Western diets have an excess of calcium relative to magnesium.
  • Magnesium + Vitamin B12: Both support neurological function. Combined deficiency is common in older adults and can worsen fatigue and cognitive symptoms.

  2. Veronese N et al. Effect of magnesium supplementation on blood pressure: a meta-analysis of randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trials. Eur J Clin Nutr. 2016;70(12):1354–1360. PMID 27402922 Meta-analysis of 34 RCTs: magnesium supplementation significantly reduced systolic BP by 2 mmHg and diastolic by 1.78 mmHg.
  3. Arab A et al. The Role of Magnesium in Sleep Health: a Systematic Review of Available Literature. Biol Trace Elem Res. 2023;201(1):121–128. PMID 35184264 Systematic review confirming associations between magnesium intake and sleep quality, with RCT evidence for benefit in older adults with insomnia.
  4. Moshfegh M et al. Oral magnesium supplementation for insomnia in older adults: a Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis. BMC Complement Med Ther. 2021;21(1):125. PMID 33865376 Meta-analysis of three RCTs in older adults: magnesium supplementation reduced sleep onset latency by 17 minutes versus placebo.
  5. Castiglioni S et al. Impact of Magnesium Supplementation on Blood Pressure: An Umbrella Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials. Nutrients. 2024;16(17):2996. PMID 39280209 Umbrella meta-analysis confirming clinically meaningful blood pressure reductions with magnesium, especially in hypertensive and magnesium-deficient individuals.
