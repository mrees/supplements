Beta-Alanine

Raises muscle carnosine to buffer acidity and extend exercise endurance

Endurance Muscle Exercise Performance Anti-fatigue
Evidence strength:
Strong (endurance exercise performance); emerging (functional performance in seniors)

Overview

Beta-alanine is a non-essential amino acid that is the rate-limiting precursor to carnosine, a dipeptide stored in high concentrations in skeletal muscle. Carnosine acts as an intramuscular buffer — it neutralises the hydrogen ions (acid) produced during intense exercise, delaying muscle fatigue and extending time to exhaustion.

Unlike most amino acids, beta-alanine is not incorporated into proteins. Its primary role is as a carnosine precursor. Supplementation over 4–12 weeks reliably raises muscle carnosine levels by 40–80%, improving the muscle's capacity to sustain high-intensity effort.

Older adults have lower muscle carnosine levels than younger adults — partly due to lower dietary intake (carnosine is found almost exclusively in animal protein) and partly due to age-related muscle changes. This makes supplementation particularly relevant in older, active individuals.

Key fact for seniors Muscle carnosine declines with age, contributing to greater fatiguability during exercise. Beta-alanine supplementation in older adults improves performance on tasks requiring sustained moderate-to-high effort (e.g., stair climbing, brisk walking, resistance training sets) and may reduce perceived fatigue during daily activities.

What the Evidence Shows

Exercise performance (general adults)

A large meta-analysis (Hobson et al., 2012; updated analyses through 2020) confirms that beta-alanine improves exercise performance in efforts lasting 1–10 minutes — the range most relevant to activities seniors undertake during exercise classes, swimming, cycling, or brisk walking. It is less effective for brief maximal efforts (<60 seconds) or prolonged low-intensity exercise (>25 minutes).

Functional performance in older adults

Several trials in older adults show improvements in 6-minute walk test distance, stair climbing power, and time to fatigue during functional tasks. These are clinically meaningful outcomes for maintaining independence and quality of life.

Muscle carnosine and antioxidant effects

Carnosine has antioxidant and anti-glycation properties (it reduces the formation of advanced glycation end-products, which accumulate in ageing tissues). While the clinical significance of these effects is still under investigation, they may provide secondary benefits beyond exercise performance.

Cognition

Carnosine is also found in the brain. Preliminary evidence suggests that beta-alanine supplementation may improve certain cognitive tasks under fatigue, though this evidence is early-stage and not yet practice-changing.

Safety & Cautions

Paraesthesia (tingling) Beta-alanine causes a characteristic harmless tingling or flushing sensation (paraesthesia) in the skin, typically in the face, neck, and hands, peaking 15–30 minutes after ingestion. This is not dangerous but can be uncomfortable. It is reduced by splitting doses throughout the day or using sustained-release formulations. The sensation diminishes with continued use.

Beta-alanine has no known serious adverse effects and no established drug interactions. It is considered safe for older adults. Those with a seafood allergy (carnosine is found in fish) should note this, though allergic reactions to beta-alanine supplementation are rare.

Synergistic Combinations

  • Beta-Alanine + Creatine: The most established exercise supplement combination. Creatine supports power and muscle mass while beta-alanine improves endurance and delays fatigue. They work through completely different mechanisms and have additive effects on overall exercise performance and capacity. This combination is particularly valuable for older adults who exercise regularly.
  • Beta-Alanine + Nitrates: Nitrates improve blood flow and oxygen delivery while beta-alanine buffers muscle acidity. Together they target different limiting factors in exercise performance.

  1. Hobson RM et al. Effects of β-alanine supplementation on exercise performance: a meta-analysis. Amino Acids. 2012;43(1):25–37. PMID 22270875 Foundational meta-analysis of 15 studies: beta-alanine significantly improved exercise capacity for efforts lasting 1–4 minutes, with strong effects on time-to-exhaustion tasks.
  2. Saunders B et al. β-alanine supplementation to improve exercise capacity and performance: a systematic review and meta-analysis. Br J Sports Med. 2017;51(8):658–669. PMID 27797728 Updated meta-analysis of 40 studies (1461 participants) confirming significant improvement in exercise capacity, particularly for 0.5–10 minute efforts — most relevant to seniors' functional exercise range.
  3. Varanoske AN et al. Twenty-four Weeks of β-Alanine Supplementation on Carnosine Content, Related Genes, and Exercise. Med Sci Sports Exerc. 2017;49(5):896–906. PMID 28157726 RCT showing 24 weeks of beta-alanine supplementation increased muscle carnosine by 55% and significantly improved high-intensity cycling capacity.
  4. Ducker KJ et al. Beta-Alanine for Improving Exercise Capacity, Muscle Strength, and Functional Performance of Older Adults: A Systematic Review. Nutrients. 2024;17(1):47. PMID 39724872 Systematic review focused on older adults: beta-alanine improved exercise capacity and reduced fatigue in functional performance tasks in several RCTs in seniors.
  5. Mendes EV et al. Role of β-Alanine Supplementation on Cognitive Function, Mood, and Physical Function in Older Adults. Nutrients. 2023;15(4):923. doi:10.3390/nu15040923 Double-blind RCT in older adults showing that beta-alanine supplementation improved several cognitive and physical function measures versus placebo over 12 weeks.
