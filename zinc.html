Zinc

Essential trace mineral for immunity, wound healing, and sensory function

Immune Function Wound Healing Taste & Smell Antioxidant
Evidence strength:
Moderate (immune, wound healing); evidence in deficient populations is strong

Overview

Zinc is an essential trace mineral involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions. It plays a central role in immune cell development, wound healing, protein synthesis, DNA synthesis, and the senses of taste and smell. Zinc cannot be stored in the body, so regular dietary intake is essential.

Rich food sources include red meat, shellfish (particularly oysters), poultry, beans, nuts, and whole grains. Absorption from plant sources is reduced by phytates, which makes vegetarians and older adults at higher risk of marginal deficiency.

Key fact for seniors Zinc deficiency is estimated to affect 30–40% of older adults. Age-related decline in zinc status contributes to immunosenescence (the age-related decline in immune function), impaired wound healing, and the loss of taste and smell common in older age.

What the Evidence Shows

Immune function and infection

Zinc is essential for the development and function of T-cells, natural killer cells, and neutrophils. Supplementation in deficient older adults improves multiple markers of immune function. Meta-analyses show that zinc lozenges (≥75 mg/day of elemental zinc) reduce the duration of the common cold by approximately 1–2 days when started within 24 hours of symptom onset.

Wound healing

Zinc is required for collagen synthesis and cell proliferation. Deficiency impairs wound healing, and supplementation in those who are deficient accelerates recovery. Zinc sulphate supplements are used clinically for leg ulcers and pressure sores in nutritionally depleted patients.

Age-related macular degeneration (AMD)

The AREDS trial established that high-dose zinc (80 mg elemental zinc) combined with antioxidant vitamins reduces progression to advanced AMD in those with intermediate disease. This is one of the most evidence-based uses of zinc supplementation.

Taste and smell

Zinc is essential for the renewal of taste receptor cells. Zinc deficiency is a recognised cause of hypogeusia (reduced taste) and hyposmia (reduced smell). Supplementation in deficient individuals can partially restore these senses.

Safety & Cautions

Important caution Long-term high-dose zinc (above 40 mg/day) competitively inhibits copper absorption and can cause copper-deficiency anaemia and neurological problems. Anyone taking therapeutic doses of zinc should also take a copper supplement (1–2 mg/day). Nausea is common when taking zinc on an empty stomach; take with food.
Drug interactions Zinc reduces absorption of fluoroquinolone and tetracycline antibiotics, as well as bisphosphonates — take at least 2 hours apart. Zinc may interfere with iron absorption when taken simultaneously; separate by 2 hours. ACE inhibitors and thiazide diuretics may increase urinary zinc loss.

Synergistic Combinations

  • Zinc + Vitamin D: Both support immune function through different but complementary mechanisms. Combined supplementation may be more effective for immune health in deficient older adults than either alone.
  • Zinc + Copper (2:1 ratio approximately): Always pair high-dose zinc with copper to prevent copper deficiency. This is not optional at doses above 25 mg/day.
  • Zinc + Antioxidants (AREDS formula): In AMD, zinc is used alongside vitamins C, E, and beta-carotene (or lutein/zeaxanthin) for maximum retinal protection.
