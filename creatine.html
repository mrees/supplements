Creatine

A well-evidenced supplement for muscle preservation and brain health — underutilised in seniors

Muscle Mass Strength Bone Health Brain Falls Prevention
Evidence strength:
Very strong (muscle/strength); emerging (bone, cognition)

Overview

Creatine is a naturally occurring compound synthesised in the liver from amino acids glycine and arginine. It is stored in muscle as phosphocreatine and serves as a rapid energy buffer for high-intensity activity. Small amounts are found in red meat and fish, but dietary intake provides far less than what can be achieved through supplementation.

Creatine monohydrate is the most studied, most affordable, and most effective supplemental form. Despite a reputation as a "bodybuilder's supplement," creatine has a growing and compelling evidence base for older adults — particularly for preventing sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss) and maintaining functional independence.

Key fact for seniors Older adults have lower baseline muscle creatine stores than younger adults. Creatine supplementation combined with resistance exercise produces greater gains in muscle mass and strength in older adults than exercise alone. Even without exercise, creatine may reduce falls and improve functional performance.

What the Evidence Shows

Muscle mass and strength (sarcopenia prevention)

This is creatine's strongest evidence base. Multiple meta-analyses of randomised controlled trials in older adults (typically 60–85 years) consistently show that creatine supplementation combined with resistance training significantly increases lean muscle mass and upper and lower body strength compared to placebo + training. Effect sizes are clinically meaningful.

Functional performance and falls

Improved muscle strength translates to better functional performance: faster walking speed, greater ability to rise from a chair, and improved balance. Several trials report a reduction in falls and fall-related injuries with creatine supplementation in older adults.

Bone health

Creatine increases the activity of osteoblasts (bone-forming cells) and appears to improve bone mineral density, particularly at the femoral neck and lumbar spine, in postmenopausal women when combined with resistance exercise. This benefit is additional to that seen with calcium and vitamin D.

Cognitive function

The brain uses phosphocreatine as an energy buffer, similar to muscle. Creatine supplementation improves working memory, reasoning, and processing speed, particularly in older adults and in those experiencing sleep deprivation or mental fatigue. Effects are more pronounced in vegetarians, who have lower baseline brain creatine levels.

Dosage by Age Group

GroupRecommended Daily DoseNotes
Younger adults (performance)3–5 g/dayNo loading phase necessary; daily maintenance works well
Seniors — muscle/bone preservation3–5 g/dayTake daily, ideally with a meal containing carbohydrate and protein
Seniors — cognitive support3–5 g/daySame dose; brain benefits seen at maintenance levels
Loading phase (optional)20 g/day for 5–7 daysSpeeds up muscle saturation but not required; may cause GI upset

Creatine monohydrate is the recommended form. Micronised creatine monohydrate dissolves more easily in water. There is no evidence that more expensive forms (creatine HCl, buffered creatine) are more effective.

Safety & Cautions

Safety profile Creatine is one of the most extensively studied sports supplements and has an excellent long-term safety record. It causes water retention within muscle cells (not subcutaneous), which may increase body weight by 1–2 kg initially. This is normal. Concerns about kidney damage are not supported by evidence in healthy individuals; however, those with pre-existing kidney disease should consult their physician before use.
Considerations Creatine supplementation may slightly raise serum creatinine levels — a routine kidney function marker — which can be misinterpreted as kidney impairment. Inform your doctor if you take creatine and have kidney function tests. No clinically significant drug interactions are established.

Synergistic Combinations

  • Creatine + Vitamin D: Both independently support muscle strength and bone health in older adults. Preliminary evidence suggests additive benefit for falls prevention and functional performance.
  • Creatine + Calcium + Vitamin D: The trio for comprehensive bone protection — creatine adds osteoblast-stimulating effects on top of the standard calcium/vitamin D combination.
  • Creatine + Beta-Alanine: Complementary mechanisms in muscle — creatine supports high-intensity power while beta-alanine buffers muscle acidity to extend endurance. Together they may improve overall exercise performance and tolerance in active seniors.
  • Creatine + Protein (whey or equivalent): Combining creatine with post-exercise protein maximises muscle protein synthesis. Carbohydrate alongside creatine improves its uptake into muscle cells.
